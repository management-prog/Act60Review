## PAGE 3 — Errors 1-3

### Error #1: Transfer Pricing That Doesn't Hold Up

**What happens:** Decree holders set service fees between their mainland entity and PR entity without a formal transfer pricing study. The IRS reclassifies income as US-sourced, eliminating the Act 60 benefit entirely.

**What the IRS looks for:** Intercompany agreements without arm's-length pricing documentation, fees that don't reflect actual economic substance, or no transfer pricing study at all.

**What to look for:** Commission an independent transfer pricing study (Section 482 compliant). Document the methodology. Update annually as the business evolves. If your current arrangement was set up informally, get it formalized before filing season.

---

### Error #2: FBAR and FATCA Reporting Gaps

**What happens:** Decree holders with foreign financial accounts (including crypto exchanges, offshore brokerage accounts, or trust accounts) fail to file FinCEN 114 (FBAR) or Form 8938 (FATCA). These are separate from the tax return and carry their own penalty regime.

**What the IRS looks for:** Unreported foreign accounts with aggregate balances exceeding $10,000 at any point during the year. Missing Form 8938 when foreign asset thresholds are met.

**What to look for:** Audit every account you hold outside the US — bank accounts, brokerage, crypto exchanges, trust distributions. File delinquent FBARs through the IRS Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures if you qualify. Penalties for willful non-filing can reach $100,000 or 50% of the account balance per violation.

---

### Error #3: Failing the Bona Fide Residency Test

**What happens:** The decree holder doesn't actually meet the requirements to be treated as a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico under IRC Section 937. Without qualifying as a PR resident, the entire Act 60 benefit is void.

**What the IRS looks for:** Three tests must be satisfied — the Presence Test (183 days in PR), the Tax Home Test (principal place of business in PR), and the Closer Connection Test (stronger ties to PR than anywhere else). The IRS cross-references travel records, credit card transactions, social media, and voter registration.

**What to look for:** Track your days meticulously using a contemporaneous log. Maintain PR voter registration, driver's license, and bank accounts. Ensure your primary business operations are genuinely conducted from PR. If you're borderline on days, adjust your travel schedule before year-end.

## PAGE 4 — Errors 4-6

### Error #4: Pre-Move Appreciation Mistakes

**What happens:** Capital gains on assets acquired before moving to PR are NOT eligible for Act 60's 0% rate. Only appreciation occurring after establishing bona fide residency qualifies. Many decree holders (and their CPAs) fail to properly bifurcate pre-move vs. post-move gains.

**What the IRS looks for:** Sales of pre-move assets reported at 0% without proper basis adjustment. No documentation of fair market value at the time of the move.

**What to look for:** Get a formal appraisal or valuation of all significant assets as of your PR residency start date. When you sell, only the post-move appreciation qualifies for the preferential rate. Pre-move gains are taxed at regular federal rates. Document this bifurcation clearly in your return.

---

### Error #5: IRC 933 Exclusion Errors

**What happens:** IRC Section 933 allows bona fide PR residents to exclude PR-source income from federal taxation. But it must be properly claimed, and only applies to income genuinely sourced to PR. Overclaiming the exclusion or miscategorizing income source triggers immediate IRS attention.

**What the IRS looks for:** Income excluded under IRC 933 that doesn't have clear PR sourcing. Federal returns that show minimal income while PR returns show substantial exempt income. Inconsistencies between federal Form 1040 and PR Planilla.

**What to look for:** Reconcile your federal and PR returns side by side. Every dollar of income should be accounted for in one jurisdiction or the other. Source determination must follow IRC sourcing rules, not just where the check was deposited.

---

### Error #6: Missing or Late Forms

**What happens:** Act 60 compliance requires more than just the standard tax return. Decree holders often miss required informative returns, annual reports to DDEC, or filing deadlines specific to PR.

**What the IRS looks for:** Missing Form 8898 (Statement for Individuals Who Begin or End Bona Fide Residence in a US Possession). Failure to file the Annual Report to DDEC by the July 15 deadline. Missing Schedule D details for exempt capital gains.

**What to look for:** Build a compliance calendar. PR filing deadlines differ from federal deadlines. The DDEC Annual Report, Hacienda estimated payments, and federal extensions all have different due dates. Missing any one of them can put your decree status at risk.

## PAGE 5 — Errors 7-9

### Error #7: Federal-PR Return Inconsistencies

**What happens:** The IRS cross-references your federal 1040 with your PR Planilla (Form 482). When the numbers don't reconcile — total income doesn't match, deductions appear on both returns, or income appears on neither — it triggers an automatic flag.

**What the IRS looks for:** Total worldwide income that doesn't add up across both returns. Deductions claimed on both federal and PR returns. Income that "disappears" between the two filings.

**What to look for:** Before filing, place your federal 1040 and PR Planilla side by side. Total income on both returns (plus any exclusions) should equal your worldwide income. No deduction should appear on both returns. Work with a CPA who understands both filing systems.

---

### Error #8: Decree Term Violations

**What happens:** Your Act 60 decree has specific terms — employment requirements, charitable donation obligations, minimum annual reporting, and property purchase deadlines. Violating any term can result in decree revocation, retroactively exposing years of income to full taxation.

**What the IRS looks for:** The IRS coordinates with DDEC and Hacienda. If your decree is revoked or suspended, the 0% rate evaporates for all open tax years.

**What to look for:** Pull your actual decree document (not what you remember it saying). Verify you've met every condition for every year. Common misses: the charitable donation requirement ($10,000/year minimum to PR nonprofits) and the employment creation requirement. If you're behind, cure the deficiency before the next annual report.

---

### Error #9: Capital Gains Sourcing Errors

**What happens:** Not all capital gains are PR-sourced just because you live in PR. The sourcing rules for capital gains depend on the type of asset, where it was purchased, and the residence of the seller at various points. Getting this wrong means claiming 0% on gains that should be taxed federally.

**What the IRS looks for:** Stock sales reported as PR-source (stocks follow the tax home of the seller, which helps — but only for post-move appreciation). Real estate gains on mainland US property claimed as PR-source (they're US-source regardless of your residence). Partnership interest sales with complex sourcing rules.

**What to look for:** Apply the correct sourcing rules for each asset class. Personal property (stocks) generally follows the seller's residence. Real property follows situs (location). Partnership interests follow a look-through analysis. When in doubt, source conservatively and document your reasoning.

## PAGE 6 — Error 10

### Error #10: Working with the Wrong CPA

**What happens:** This may be the most costly error of all. Many decree holders use a CPA who is competent in general US tax preparation but lacks specific expertise in Act 60, IRC 937, Puerto Rico tax law, and the intersection of federal-PR compliance. The result: returns that look clean but contain fundamental structural errors.

**What the IRS looks for:** Returns prepared by firms with no documented Act 60 expertise. Boilerplate positions that don't reflect the individual's actual situation. Missing specialized forms and disclosures.

**What to look for:** Your CPA should be able to answer these questions without hesitation:
- How do you bifurcate pre-move and post-move capital gains?
- What's your approach to transfer pricing documentation?
- How do you reconcile the federal 1040 with the PR Planilla?
- Have you represented clients in a Campaign 685 examination?

If they can't, it may be time for a second opinion — before the IRS provides one for you.
