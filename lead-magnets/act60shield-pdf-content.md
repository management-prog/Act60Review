## PAGE 2 — Situation Overview

**Heading:** Campaign 685 Is Not a Rumor. It's Operational.

The IRS Large Business & International (LB&I) division launched Campaign 685 — formally titled "Individuals Claiming Benefits Under IRC 937" — to examine taxpayers who claim tax benefits as bona fide residents of US possessions, with a primary focus on Puerto Rico Act 60 decree holders.

**By the numbers:**

| Metric | Figure |
|--------|--------|
| Active investigations | 100+ |
| Average deficiency per examined return | $287,000 |
| Penalty rate on assessed deficiencies | 40% |
| Total claimed exempt income under review | $2.3 billion |
| Campaign status | Active and expanding |

*Source: GAO Report GAO-26-107225*

This is not a limited pilot. Campaign 685 has dedicated staff, allocated budget, and a systematic approach to identifying examination targets. Understanding what they look for — and how examinations typically proceed — is the first step in maintaining a strong compliance position.

## PAGE 3 — How Targets Are Selected

**Heading:** Target Selection: What Triggers an Examination

Based on publicly available IRS guidance, GAO reports, and observed enforcement patterns, Campaign 685 examinations appear to be initiated through several channels:

**Data Matching**
The IRS cross-references federal Form 1040 filings with PR Planilla (Form 482) data, DDEC annual reports, and FinCEN filings. Inconsistencies between these documents can generate automated flags.

**Income Threshold Screening**
Returns claiming large IRC 933 exclusions — particularly those with significant capital gains or service income reported at preferential rates — appear to receive heightened scrutiny.

**Referral from Other Examinations**
If a related entity, business partner, or co-investor is examined and issues are identified, the examination may expand to connected decree holders.

**Information Document Requests (IDRs)**
The IRS has been issuing broad IDRs to decree holders requesting:
- Complete travel logs and supporting documentation
- Intercompany agreements and transfer pricing studies
- Evidence of PR economic substance (leases, employee records, utility bills)
- Bank and brokerage account statements
- DDEC annual reports and correspondence

**Public Information**
Social media activity, property records, voter registration, and other publicly available data may be reviewed to assess residency claims.

## PAGE 4 — The 6 Primary Examination Areas

**Heading:** What They Examine: The 6 Focus Areas

Based on reported examination patterns, Campaign 685 investigations typically focus on these areas:

### 1. Residency Qualification (IRC 937)
The foundational question: Does the taxpayer actually qualify as a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico? The IRS examines the three-part test (Presence, Tax Home, Closer Connection) with particular attention to travel patterns and mainland US ties.

### 2. Income Sourcing
Is the income claimed as PR-sourced actually sourced to Puerto Rico under applicable rules? Service income, capital gains, and passive income each follow different sourcing rules. The IRS has challenged cases where income was attributed to PR without sufficient documentation.

### 3. Transfer Pricing (IRC 482)
For decree holders operating through related entities, the IRS examines whether intercompany pricing reflects arm's-length standards. Informal fee arrangements without supporting studies have been a frequent area of adjustment.

### 4. Pre-Move vs. Post-Move Capital Gains
The IRS has identified cases where decree holders reported the full gain on asset sales at the preferential Act 60 rate without properly bifurcating pre-move appreciation (taxable at federal rates) from post-move appreciation (eligible for preferential treatment).

### 5. Foreign Account Compliance (FBAR/FATCA)
Decree holders with international financial footprints are examined for compliance with FinCEN 114 (FBAR), Form 8938 (FATCA), and related reporting obligations. Non-compliance in this area carries its own penalty regime independent of income tax.

### 6. Decree Term Compliance
The IRS coordinates with DDEC and Hacienda to verify that decree terms are being met — charitable donations, employment requirements, annual reporting, and property purchase deadlines.

## PAGE 5 — The Examination Timeline

**Heading:** How an Examination Typically Proceeds

Understanding the typical examination timeline can help decree holders recognize where they are in the process and what may come next.

**Phase 1: Initial Contact (Month 1)**
The IRS issues an examination notification letter, typically via certified mail. This letter identifies the tax year(s) under examination and the general areas of inquiry. An initial Information Document Request (IDR) accompanies the letter.

**Phase 2: Document Production (Months 2-4)**
The taxpayer (or their representative) responds to IDRs. The IRS may issue follow-up IDRs requesting additional documentation. Response deadlines are typically 30 days but can sometimes be extended.

**Phase 3: Substantive Examination (Months 4-8)**
The examining agent reviews documentation, may conduct interviews, and analyzes the positions taken on the return. The agent may request meetings or additional information.

**Phase 4: Proposed Adjustments (Months 8-12)**
If the IRS identifies issues, a Revenue Agent's Report (RAR) is issued detailing proposed adjustments and the basis for each. The taxpayer has 30 days to agree or protest.

**Phase 5: Resolution (Months 12-24+)**
Cases may be resolved through agreement, IRS Appeals, or Tax Court litigation. The process can extend for years depending on the complexity and amounts involved.

**Key observation:** The total elapsed time from initial contact to resolution typically ranges from 12 to 36 months. During this period, the statute of limitations on assessment may be extended by agreement.

## PAGE 6 — Documentation That Matters

**Heading:** The Documentation Standard

Examinations are won or lost on documentation. Based on publicly available examination outcomes and practitioner guidance, the following documentation categories are most frequently requested and most impactful:

**Tier 1 — Critical (requested in virtually every examination)**
- Contemporaneous presence test log with corroborating evidence
- Transfer pricing study (if intercompany transactions exist)
- Federal-PR return reconciliation workpaper
- Pre-move asset fair market valuations
- DDEC annual reports (all years)

**Tier 2 — Important (commonly requested)**
- Intercompany agreements and service contracts
- Evidence of PR economic substance (lease, utility bills, employee records)
- Bank and brokerage statements (US and foreign)
- Travel records (flights, hotel receipts, passport stamps)
- Charitable donation receipts (PR nonprofits)

**Tier 3 — Supporting (may be requested)**
- Social media records
- Cell phone location data
- Vehicle registration and insurance
- Medical and dental records (PR providers)
- Children's school enrollment (PR schools)

> **The standard is contemporaneous documentation.** Records created at the time of the event carry significantly more weight than records reconstructed after an examination begins.

## PAGE 7 — Defensive Posture: 5 Principles

**Heading:** Maintaining a Strong Compliance Position

These five principles are drawn from publicly available IRS guidance, Tax Court opinions, and practitioner best practices. They represent general observations, not specific recommendations for any individual situation.

**Principle 1: Document in Real Time**
Contemporaneous records — created at or near the time of the event — are the strongest form of evidence. A presence log maintained daily carries more weight than a spreadsheet reconstructed from memory two years later.

**Principle 2: Reconcile Before Filing**
Federal and PR returns should tell the same story from two different angles. Income, deductions, and exclusions should reconcile across both returns. Inconsistencies that are visible to you are also visible to IRS data-matching systems.

**Principle 3: Treat Transfer Pricing as Foundational**
If your business structure involves intercompany transactions, a formal transfer pricing study is not optional — it's the foundation of your entire Act 60 position. Informal arrangements without documentation have been a primary area of IRS adjustment.

**Principle 4: Maintain Substance Over Form**
The IRS examines whether economic substance matches the tax position. A PR entity that reports significant income should have corresponding operational infrastructure in PR — employees, office space, equipment, and demonstrable business activity.

**Principle 5: Engage Qualified Professionals**
Act 60 compliance sits at the intersection of federal tax law, Puerto Rico tax law, international reporting requirements, and decree-specific obligations. This intersection requires specialized expertise. General tax preparation experience, while valuable, may not cover all the relevant areas.
