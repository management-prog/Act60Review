PAGE 3 -- ERRORS 1-3

Error #1: Transfer Pricing That Does Not Hold Up
What happens: Decree holders set service fees between mainland and PR entities without a formal transfer pricing study. The IRS reclassifies income as US-sourced, eliminating the Act 60 benefit.
What the IRS looks for: Intercompany agreements without arm's-length pricing, fees without economic substance, no TP study.
What to look for: Whether a current independent TP study (Section 482 compliant) exists, whether methodology is documented, whether it is updated annually.

Error #2: FBAR and FATCA Reporting Gaps
What happens: Decree holders with foreign accounts (crypto exchanges, offshore brokerage, trust accounts) fail to file FinCEN 114 or Form 8938.
What the IRS looks for: Unreported accounts exceeding $10,000 aggregate. Missing Form 8938 at threshold.
What to look for: Whether every account held outside the US has been identified. Penalties for willful non-filing can reach $100,000 or 50% of account balance.

Error #3: Failing the Bona Fide Residency Test
What happens: The decree holder does not meet IRC 937 requirements. Without qualifying, the entire Act 60 benefit is void.
What the IRS looks for: Three tests -- Presence (183 days), Tax Home, Closer Connection. Cross-references travel records, credit cards, social media, voter registration.
What to look for: Whether a contemporaneous log exists, whether PR voter registration/license/bank accounts are maintained, whether business operations are genuinely in PR.

PAGE 4 -- ERRORS 4-6

Error #4: Pre-Move Appreciation Mistakes
What happens: Capital gains on pre-move assets reported at 0% without bifurcating pre-move vs. post-move appreciation. Only post-move appreciation qualifies.
What the IRS looks for: Full gain at preferential rate without basis adjustment. No FMV documentation at move date.
What to look for: Whether formal valuations exist as of PR residency start date. Whether the return properly separates pre-move (federal rates) from post-move appreciation.

Error #5: IRC 933 Exclusion Errors
What happens: IRC 933 excludes PR-source income from federal tax, but only for genuinely PR-sourced income. Overclaiming triggers IRS attention.
What the IRS looks for: Excluded income without clear PR sourcing. Minimal federal income but substantial PR exempt income.
What to look for: Whether federal and PR returns reconcile. Whether every dollar is accounted for in one jurisdiction.

Error #6: Missing or Late Forms
What happens: Required informative returns, DDEC annual reports, or PR-specific deadlines are missed.
What the IRS looks for: Missing Form 8898. Missing DDEC Annual Report (July 15 deadline). Missing Schedule D details.
What to look for: Whether a compliance calendar exists covering all PR and federal deadlines.

PAGE 5 -- ERRORS 7-9

Error #7: Federal-PR Return Inconsistencies
What happens: IRS cross-references 1040 with Planilla (482). Numbers that do not reconcile trigger automatic flags.
What the IRS looks for: Income that does not add up across returns. Double-claimed deductions. Disappearing income.
What to look for: Whether total income on both returns plus exclusions equals worldwide income.

Error #8: Decree Term Violations
What happens: Employment requirements, charitable donations ($10,000/yr to PR nonprofits), annual reporting, or property purchase deadlines are missed. Violation can result in revocation.
What the IRS looks for: Coordinates with DDEC/Hacienda. Revocation eliminates preferential rate for all open years.
What to look for: Whether the actual decree document has been reviewed recently and every condition met.

Error #9: Capital Gains Sourcing Errors
What happens: Not all gains are PR-sourced just because the seller lives in PR. Different asset classes follow different rules.
What the IRS looks for: Stocks reported as PR-source without analysis. Mainland real estate gains claimed as PR-source. Partnership sales with complex sourcing.
What to look for: Correct sourcing per asset class (personal property follows seller residence, real property follows situs, partnerships follow look-through).

PAGE 6 -- ERROR 10

Error #10: Working with the Wrong CPA
What happens: CPA competent in general US tax but lacking Act 60/IRC 937/PR tax expertise. Returns look clean but contain structural errors.
What the IRS looks for: Returns from firms without documented Act 60 expertise. Boilerplate positions.
What to look for: Whether the CPA can explain pre-move/post-move bifurcation, TP documentation approach, federal-PR reconciliation, and Campaign 685 examination experience.
